Selling refurbished laptops to students: lessons from the hustle

How running a side business taught me more about product-market fit and user psychology than any course I've taken.

I started selling laptops by accident. A friend needed one urgently before resumption and couldn't afford the prices in computer village. I helped him source one at a better rate, handled the negotiation, tested it before handover — and he paid me a small finder's fee on top.

Three weeks later, two of his friends contacted me with the same request. That's when I realised this wasn't a one-time favour. It was a market.

A year in, I've sold to over 200 students across Lagos campuses. Here's what the business actually taught me — lessons that go far beyond "buy low, sell high."

Lesson 1: Know your customer better than they know themselves

Most students think they know what laptop they want. They'll come to you and say "I want an i7 with 16GB RAM." But when you ask what they're going to use it for, it's usually: schoolwork, browsing, maybe some light coding, and Netflix.

For that use case, a well-maintained i5 with 8GB RAM and an SSD runs absolutely fine — and costs ₦40,000–₦60,000 less. The student who insisted on an i7 for "future-proofing" is the same student who blows their budget and comes back asking for installment plans.

The insight: People buy specs they don't need because specs feel safe and tangible. Your job as a seller is to translate what they actually need into what they should actually buy. Customers who trust your advice come back. Customers who feel sold to don't.

Lesson 2: Trust is the product, the laptop is just the delivery

Computer Village in Lagos has plenty of laptops. What it often doesn't have — especially for a student walking in alone — is someone they trust to tell them the truth about what they're buying.

My edge was never price. There are sellers with lower prices than me. My edge was that students knew I would test the device properly, tell them about any issues before they paid, and be reachable after the sale if something went wrong. That reputation, built one transaction at a time, became the actual business.

Every referral I've received came from a customer who felt they could trust me. Not one came from someone who just found me cheapest.

Lesson 3: The post-sale experience is where loyalty is built

Most sellers disappear after the money is collected. I made a deliberate choice to do the opposite — follow up two days after delivery, answer questions about setup and software, and be available on WhatsApp if something came up.

This sounds simple. It cost me very little in time. But it created a disproportionate number of repeat customers and referrals. One customer who bought a ₦85,000 laptop from me has since sent me five people. That's over ₦400,000 in sales from one relationship maintained with good follow-up.

This translates directly to web development: The clients who come back — and refer others — are the ones who felt supported after the project was delivered. Checking in post-launch, fixing small things without invoicing, being genuinely available — these build the kind of loyalty no proposal wins.

The seven lessons, summarised

01
Understand what people actually need, not just what they ask for
Ask questions before presenting options. The right product for the customer builds trust; the expensive product they didn't need destroys it.
02
Trust is your competitive advantage in a commoditised market
When everyone sells the same product, the one who wins is the one the customer believes. Build that through honesty, not marketing.
03
Follow up after every sale
Two messages — one the day after, one a week later — is enough to turn a one-time customer into a long-term one.
04
Your reputation travels faster than your marketing
Every campus is a small world. One bad experience spreads. One exceptional experience spreads further.
05
Cash flow matters more than profit margin
A laptop sitting unsold is capital you can't use. Moving stock at a slightly lower margin beats holding out for the perfect price.
06
Clear communication prevents most disputes
Every problem I've had with a customer came from something unclear at the point of sale. State what's included, what's not, and what the warranty covers — in writing, even if it's a WhatsApp message.
07
Running a business teaches you things classrooms can't
Negotiation, pricing psychology, customer communication, inventory management — I learned all of these through doing, not studying.

How this changed how I think about software

The most unexpected outcome of running this business was how it changed my approach to building products. Before, I thought about features. Now I think about users.

When I built the CGPA calculator, I didn't start with the code. I started by thinking about the student sitting with a paper and calculator making errors at 11pm before exams. When I built the laptop store landing page, I thought about the parent scrolling on their phone trying to decide whether this seller can be trusted.

Good products — like good business relationships — are built around the person on the other side, not the thing you're selling. That's the lesson the hustle taught me, and it's the one I carry into every project I work on now.
